# Project Overview Proposal (V4)

## 1. Introduction

This project focuses on the development of an application-stage credit risk scoring framework using the Home Credit Default Risk dataset. The central aim is to support better lending decisions by identifying applicants who are more likely to default and by converting model outputs into interpretable risk scores and rating bands that can be used in a business setting. From a business perspective, the project is intended to improve approval quality, reduce expected credit losses, strengthen portfolio segmentation, and support more consistent underwriting decisions. From a technical perspective, the project aims to construct a customer-level analytical dataset from multiple related tables, engineer a structured set of traditional, behaviour-enhanced, and proxy alternative features, compare multiple predictive models, and evaluate model quality through discrimination, calibration, interpretability, fairness, and practical usability.

The project will adopt a staged modelling approach. It will begin with a baseline model built on traditional application-stage features, then extend the analysis by incorporating aggregated historical credit and behavioural features that would plausibly be available at the time of application. These enhancements are not meant to represent post-loan future behaviour, but rather prior repayment, bureau, and credit usage histories summarised into customer-level indicators. In addition, selected non-traditional variables in the training data, such as social-risk exposure indicators and contact-stability proxies, will be treated as proxy alternative features rather than true external alternative data. This allows the project to study whether richer risk signals beyond basic demographic and financial variables can improve application-stage scoring while remaining conceptually honest about the data source.

## 2. Industry Overview

The project is situated in the consumer lending, retail banking, and fintech credit risk industry. Institutions operating in this space must decide whether an applicant should be approved, rejected, priced differently, or referred for manual review. The quality of this decision is fundamental because poor underwriting can lead to higher default rates, weaker portfolio quality, and lower profitability, while overly conservative approval strategies may suppress growth and reduce access to potentially creditworthy borrowers.

In practice, lenders traditionally rely on structured application data, bureau information, and historical repayment records to estimate risk at the point of application. Over time, however, the industry has increasingly incorporated richer feature engineering, behavioural summaries, machine learning models, and governance tools such as explainability and fairness diagnostics. Some lenders also use broader alternative data sources, such as telecom, utility, or digital footprint data, particularly when applicants have thin or incomplete credit files. The current project does not use such external alternative data directly, but it does contain variables that can act as proxy alternative features, for example social surroundings delinquency indicators and phone-change recency variables. This makes the project relevant to modern credit risk practice while preserving a clear distinction between traditional structured credit variables and broader non-traditional proxy signals.

## 3. Business Problem & Objectives

The core business problem is how to identify high-risk borrowers more accurately at the point of application so that lending decisions are commercially sound, operationally practical, and sufficiently transparent. This problem is critical because lenders must balance growth and risk at the same time. Approving too many risky borrowers increases bad debt and weakens portfolio quality, while rejecting too many potentially good borrowers reduces revenue opportunities and may damage competitiveness.

Several business questions arise from this problem. First, the lender needs to know which applicants are most likely to default and whether they can be reliably separated from lower-risk borrowers before approval. Second, the lender needs to determine whether richer historical credit and behavioural information can improve prediction beyond static application variables alone. Third, the lender needs to understand whether selected non-traditional proxy variables, such as social-risk exposure and contact-stability indicators, add incremental predictive value. Fourth, the lender needs outputs that can be operationalised, such as scores, rating bands, and thresholds that could support approval, rejection, or manual review decisions. These questions matter because they determine whether the project remains only an academic modelling exercise or becomes a framework that resembles practical underwriting support.

The business objectives of the project are therefore to improve applicant risk classification, reduce expected credit losses, support more disciplined loan approval decisions, and segment borrowers into categories that are meaningful for underwriting. The technical objectives are aligned directly to these goals. Technically, the project seeks to integrate multi-table credit data into a consistent customer-level modelling base, engineer and compare three broad feature families, and evaluate whether each family contributes additional signal. These feature families are: traditional features, which include standard application, income, demographic, and core credit variables; behaviour-enhanced features, which summarise historical repayment, utilisation, delinquency, and application patterns available at the decision point; and proxy alternative features, which capture non-traditional signals such as social surroundings delinquency observations and phone-change recency. The project also aims to compare multiple models, assess their predictive and business performance, and produce a score-oriented framework that can support application-stage risk assessment in a more interpretable form.

To summarise the alignment between business and technical objectives more clearly, the table below presents the two dimensions side by side:

| Business Objectives | Technical Objectives |
|---|---|
| Improve applicant risk classification. | Build a customer-level modelling base and develop models that distinguish higher-risk from lower-risk applicants. |
| Reduce expected credit losses. | Engineer and evaluate traditional, behaviour-enhanced, and proxy alternative features to improve predictive strength. |
| Support more disciplined loan approval decisions. | Compare multiple modelling approaches and assess their predictive and business performance. |
| Test whether historical credit and behavioural information adds value beyond static application data. | Construct and compare feature families to measure the incremental value of behavioural enhancement. |
| Test whether selected non-traditional proxy variables provide useful underwriting signal. | Incorporate proxy alternative features, such as social-risk exposure and phone-change recency, and evaluate their added predictive value. |
| Segment borrowers into meaningful underwriting categories. | Produce score-oriented outputs such as risk scores, rating bands, and decision-support thresholds. |
| Ensure that results can support practical underwriting rather than remain purely academic. | Develop an interpretable application-stage scoring framework suitable for practical decision support. |

## 4. Project Design

The project will follow a staged road map designed to move from business understanding to model interpretation in a structured way. The first stage will focus on understanding the lending context, reviewing the dataset structure, identifying the analytical unit and target definition, and clarifying which data elements are appropriate for application-stage modelling. This stage is especially important because the project aims to remain consistent with A-score logic, meaning that any behaviour-enhanced variable must represent information available before or at the application decision point rather than post-loan future outcomes.

The second stage will involve exploratory data analysis. This will include examining the target distribution, identifying missing values and outliers, reviewing variable types, and understanding how the main application table relates to bureau, repayment, and balance-history tables. During this stage, variables will also be reviewed and organised into the three feature families described above: traditional, behaviour-enhanced, and proxy alternative. This classification will help structure later experiments and make the final model comparison more meaningful.

The third stage will focus on data preparation and feature engineering. One-to-many historical tables will be aggregated into customer-level summaries, categorical and numerical variables will be cleaned and transformed, and derived features such as ratios, trends, volatility indicators, repayment consistency measures, and utilisation metrics will be created. At this stage, social surroundings delinquency variables and phone-change recency variables can be explicitly grouped into the proxy alternative feature layer. This design enables the project to compare a purely traditional model, a traditional plus behavioural model, and a traditional plus behavioural plus proxy alternative model.

The fourth stage will involve model development. A baseline Logistic Regression model will be built first to provide an interpretable benchmark. This will then be compared with stronger machine learning models such as XGBoost or LightGBM, and an optional MLP may be explored if a neural network benchmark is useful for the final narrative. The fifth stage will focus on model evaluation and interpretation. Models will be compared using predictive metrics and business-oriented measures, and the contribution of key variables will be examined using explainability techniques such as SHAP. Calibration, fairness, and, where feasible, stability considerations will also be reviewed to determine whether any gain in predictive power is practically meaningful.

The final stage will translate technical outputs into business framing. Predicted probabilities may be converted into credit scores using a PDO-based transformation, and those scores may then be grouped into rating bands to support clearer business interpretation. This final stage is intended to connect the modelling exercise back to underwriting practice by demonstrating how a predictive system can be translated into a more operationally meaningful decision-support framework.

## 5. Scope of Work

### Data Set

The project will use the Home Credit Default Risk dataset from Kaggle. The data is primarily structured tabular credit data, including application information, external bureau records, historical loan applications, instalment payments, credit card balances, and POS or cash loan balances. From a modelling perspective, the dataset supports three layers of features. The first layer is traditional credit risk data, which includes standard financial, demographic, and application variables such as income, loan amount, annuity, education, and ownership indicators. The second layer consists of behaviour-enhanced features derived from historical performance records, such as delinquency frequency, repayment consistency, utilisation behaviour, and historical application outcomes. The third layer consists of proxy alternative features found within the train dataset, particularly variables related to clients’ social surroundings delinquency observations and variables measuring how many days before application the client changed phone. These are not true external alternative data sources, but they can reasonably be treated as non-traditional proxy signals beyond standard financial variables.

The data preparation process will include handling missing values, reviewing outliers, encoding categorical variables, aligning the analytical grain at the customer level, and aggregating one-to-many historical records into customer-level summaries. Additional transformations are likely to include creating ratio variables, trend and volatility indicators, repayment discipline measures, utilisation metrics, and carefully defined proxy variables. Where appropriate, the project will also compare the predictive value of the different feature layers in order to test whether non-traditional proxy signals add meaningful incremental performance.

### Proposed Models, Techniques, and Evaluation Methodologies

The modelling strategy will begin with Logistic Regression as the main interpretable baseline and then extend to stronger machine learning models such as XGBoost or LightGBM. An MLP may be considered as an optional benchmark if a neural network comparison is useful for the project narrative. In addition to model fitting, the project may include scorecard-oriented techniques such as IV/WOE analysis where relevant, probability-to-score transformation using PDO, and explainability methods such as SHAP to understand the drivers of model predictions.

Evaluation will be carried out using both predictive and business-oriented methodologies. Predictive performance will be assessed using measures such as ROC-AUC, KS statistic, Precision, Recall, F1-score, and PR-AUC. Business relevance will be considered through score-band bad rates, lift or gain, and cutoff analysis to understand how model outputs might support lending decisions. Model quality will also be assessed through calibration analysis and fairness diagnostics, including metrics such as Disparate Impact and Equalized Odds where appropriate. If the data structure permits, some stability-oriented analysis may also be included. A key analytical comparison in the project will be whether predictive performance changes materially as the model moves from traditional features alone, to traditional plus behavioural features, and finally to traditional plus behavioural plus proxy alternative features.

## 6. Key Deliverables

The final outcomes of the project are expected to include a clearly articulated business and industry framing of the problem, a documented customer-level analytical dataset created from the multi-table source data, and a feature engineering framework that explicitly distinguishes between traditional features, behaviour-enhanced historical features, and proxy alternative features. The project is also expected to deliver a baseline interpretable model, one or more stronger machine learning comparison models, and an evaluation report that summarises predictive performance, business usefulness, and model governance findings.

In addition, the project should produce explainability outputs, fairness diagnostics, and where feasible, a score development output that maps predicted default probabilities into interpretable risk scores and rating bands. The final deliverable should therefore not be limited to a single trained model, but should take the form of a complete project package containing the problem framing, technical workflow, feature-family comparison, modelling results, and practical conclusions that could support future extension into a more formal application scorecard development exercise.
